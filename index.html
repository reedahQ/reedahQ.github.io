MY TOP FAVOURITE FRANCHISE

10. CREED:

The Creed trilogy revitalizes the Rocky legacy through the gripping journey of Adonis Creed, the son of boxing legend Apollo Creed. In Creed (2015), Adonis seeks out an aging Rocky Balboa to train him, delivering a heartfelt exploration of identity and legacy, with Michael B. Jordan's raw performance anchoring the film. Creed II (2018) raises the stakes as Adonis faces Viktor Drago, the son of the man who killed his father, blending intense fight scenes with emotional depth. Creed III (2023), directed by Jordan himself, shifts to a more personal conflict as Adonis confronts a former friend turned rival, offering a fresh narrative angle while retaining the franchise's emotional core. The trilogy skillfully balances nostalgia with new character arcs, and its visceral fight choreography, compelling performances, and emotionally resonant storytelling make it a standout in the sports drama genre, but i put this in number 10 because it struggles to step out its predecessor's- (Rocky) shadow

9. FINDING NEMO &FINDING DORY

Finding Nemo (2003) follows the journey of Marlin, an overprotective clownfish, as he crosses the ocean to rescue his son, Nemo, who was captured by a diver. Along the way, Marlin is joined by the forgetful but lovable Dory, encountering various ocean creatures in their quest. In *Finding Dory* (2016), the focus shifts to Dory, who embarks on a search for her long-lost family, with Marlin and Nemo by her side. Both films explore themes of family, perseverance, and the power of self-discovery.
While Finding Nemo is a charming and heartfelt adventure with stunning animation and memorable characters, *Finding Dory* feels more like a retread, lacking the emotional depth of the original. Though Dory is an endearing character, the sequel doesn't capture the same magic, feeling more like an extended version of the original's best moments. Still, both films are visually captivating, offering plenty of humor and heart for younger audiences.

8. TROLLS

The Trolls (2016) is a brightly colored, music-filled animated film that follows Poppy and Branch, two contrasting Trolls, as they embark on a quest to rescue their friends from the pessimistic Bergens, who believe eating Trolls brings happiness. The movie is packed with pop songs, glitter, and constant cheerfulness, aimed at a younger audience. While visually vibrant and undeniably energetic, the film’s plot is thin, relying heavily on predictable tropes and over-the-top optimism, which can feel grating. Despite a catchy soundtrack and moments of charm, *Trolls* lacks depth, offering little for adults beyond its surface-level joy, making it more style than substance.

7. FAST AND FURIOUS

The *Fast & Furious* franchise, spanning over a decade, follows Dominic Toretto (Vin Diesel) and his crew as they evolve from street racers to international operatives involved in high-octane heists, globe-trotting missions, and saving the world from increasingly absurd threats. What began as a story about underground car culture in *The Fast and the Furious* (2001) has morphed into an over-the-top action series with gravity-defying stunts, implausible plots, and a parade of returning characters. While the franchise undeniably delivers thrills and entertainment, it often feels like a hollow spectacle with little substance beneath the flashy set pieces. Character development takes a backseat to explosions, and the emotional stakes are undercut by its willingness to push believability to the extreme. For action junkies, it’s a wild ride, but for those craving depth,I believe it leaves much to be desired.

6. TRANSFORMERS

The *Transformers* series, based on the iconic toy line, follows the war between the heroic Autobots and the villainous Decepticons, sentient alien robots who can disguise themselves as vehicles. The films, starting with *Transformers* (2007) and spanning multiple sequels, center on the battle to protect Earth, with humans caught in the middle of these massive, explosive clashes. Directed primarily by Michael Bay, the series is known for its visually spectacular action scenes and cutting-edge CGI, but beyond the spectacle, the plots are often thin, the characters underdeveloped, and the dialogue clichéd. While the first film had some novelty and charm, the subsequent entries feel bloated and repetitive, relying on noisy, chaotic battles and little else. Despite its box office success, the franchise is more about spectacle than substance, leaving it hollow for those seeking more depth.

5. AMAZING SPIDERMAN

The *Amazing Spider-Man* franchise consists of two films released between 2012 and 2014, featuring Andrew Garfield as the titular character. The series reboots the Spider-Man narrative, focusing on Peter Parker’s origins, his relationship with Gwen Stacy (played by Emma Stone), and his battles against various villains, including the Lizard and Electro. The films aim to explore Peter's journey of self-discovery and responsibility while grappling with the consequences of his dual life as a superhero. However, the franchise ended abruptly after *The Amazing Spider-Man 2* (2014), which struggled to balance its ambitious narrative threads. As a film critic, the *Amazing Spider-Man* franchise feels like a missed opportunity, caught in a web of convoluted storytelling and inconsistent tone. While Andrew Garfield delivers a charming and youthful portrayal of Peter Parker, the films often lean too heavily on special effects and forget the emotional core that made the original *Spider-Man* trilogy resonate. The attempts to set up a broader universe ultimately backfire, resulting in a narrative that feels bloated and unfocused. Despite some standout moments, including the chemistry between Garfield and Stone, the franchise fails to leave a lasting impact, showcasing the challenges of rebooting such an iconic character without fully understanding what made him beloved in the first place.

4. LORD OF THE RINGS

The *Lord of the Rings* franchise, based on J.R.R. Tolkien's epic novels, chronicles the quest to destroy the One Ring, a powerful artifact sought by the dark lord Sauron. The story follows the Fellowship, led by Frodo Baggins, as they traverse the treacherous lands of Middle-earth, battling orcs, encountering mythical beings, and forging deep bonds of friendship. Peter Jackson’s film adaptation, comprising *The Fellowship of the Ring* (2001), *The Two Towers* (2002), and *The Return of the King* (2003), masterfully brings this rich world to life with stunning visuals, groundbreaking special effects, and a stirring score. However, while the films are undeniably grand in scale and ambition, they sometimes suffer from pacing issues and an over-reliance on CGI, which detracts from the intimacy of Tolkien’s narrative. Despite these flaws, the trilogy remains a monumental achievement in filmmaking, encapsulating themes of friendship, sacrifice, and the struggle between good and evil, solidifying its place as a cornerstone of modern fantasy cinema.

3. HARRY POTTER

The *Harry Potter* franchise, based on J.K. Rowling's beloved novels, spans eight films that chronicle the journey of a young wizard, Harry Potter, as he navigates the challenges of adolescence, friendship, and the fight against the dark wizard Voldemort. Beginning with *Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone* (2001), the series introduces audiences to the enchanting world of Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry and its diverse array of characters, from the loyal Hermione Granger to the conflicted Draco Malfoy. As the story progresses through themes of love, sacrifice, and the battle between good and evil, the films gradually shift from whimsical adventures to darker, more complex narratives culminating in the epic showdown in *Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows – Part 2* (2011). While the *Harry Potter* franchise has undeniably captivated audiences worldwide, its cinematic execution is uneven. The earlier films are charming and visually stunning, capturing the magic of the source material, but as the series progresses, the pacing becomes erratic, and the character development suffers, particularly in the later installments. The films often rely too heavily on special effects at the expense of storytelling depth, leaving fans longing for the intricate world-building and rich character arcs present in the books. Overall, while the franchise remains a cultural phenomenon with its share of memorable moments, it ultimately struggles to maintain the same level of enchantment and emotional resonance throughout its lengthy run.

2. AVENGERS

The *Avengers* franchise, part of the Marvel Cinematic Universe (MCU), follows a group of superheroes who unite to confront various cosmic and terrestrial threats. Beginning with *The Avengers* (2012), the team—comprising Iron Man, Captain America, Thor, Hulk, Black Widow, and Hawkeye—fights against Loki and an alien invasion. Subsequent films, including *Avengers: Age of Ultron* (2015), *Avengers: Infinity War* (2018), and *Avengers: Endgame* (2019), escalate the stakes with formidable villains like Ultron and Thanos, ultimately culminating in a climactic battle that reshapes the universe. Each installment intertwines character arcs, humor, and epic action sequences, establishing the Avengers as a cultural phenomenon. While the *Avengers* franchise has redefined blockbuster filmmaking with its ambitious crossovers and impressive visual effects, it often prioritizes spectacle over substance. The films frequently feel formulaic, relying on tried-and-true tropes of heroism and sacrifice, which can dilute the impact of character development. Although *Endgame* delivers a satisfying conclusion to the saga with emotional moments, it can’t escape the overarching sense of fatigue that comes from an overstuffed cinematic universe. Ultimately, while entertaining, the franchise sometimes sacrifices depth for spectacle, leaving critics to ponder whether its grand ambitions truly resonate with audiences or merely entertain them.

1. TWILLIGHT

The *Twilight* franchise, based on Stephenie Meyer's novels, follows the tumultuous love story between Bella Swan, a human, and Edward Cullen, a vampire, set against a backdrop of supernatural intrigue and intense romance. Beginning with *Twilight* (2008), the series introduces Bella's infatuation with Edward and the complexities of their relationship, which is further complicated by the presence of Jacob Black, a werewolf and Bella’s close friend. As the saga progresses through *New Moon* (2009), *Eclipse* (2010), and the two-part *Breaking Dawn* (2011), themes of love, sacrifice, and identity are explored, culminating in a climactic battle between vampire factions and a bittersweet resolution. While the *Twilight* franchise captivated a generation and became a cultural phenomenon, its execution often leaves much to be desired. The dialogue frequently veers into melodrama, and the pacing can feel excruciatingly slow, particularly in the middle entries. Kristen Stewart's portrayal of Bella oscillates between passivity and occasional determination, while Robert Pattinson's Edward is more brooding than compelling. Despite its shortcomings, the franchise successfully taps into themes of forbidden love and the struggle for acceptance, resonating with its audience, but ultimately, it sacrifices character depth for romantic fantasy, rendering it a mixed bag of teen angst and supernatural clichés.
